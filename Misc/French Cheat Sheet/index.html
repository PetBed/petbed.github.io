L'adjectifs

In French, adjectives are like clothes: they must "fit" the noun they describe. This means they can have up to four forms depending on the noun's gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural).

The Four Forms: petit (small)

Noun Type Adjective Form Example Meaning
Masculine singular petit un petit chien a small dog
Masculine plural petits des petits chiens small dogs
Feminine singular petite une petite fille a small girl
Feminine plural petites des petites filles small girls

Key Adjective Rules

Rule 1 & 2: Agreement (Gender & Number)

Rule 1: Add -e for feminine. (petit → petite)

↳ If it already ends in -e (like calme), it stays the same.

Rule 2: Add -s for plural. (petit → petits)

↳ If it already ends in -s or -x (like surpris), the masculine plural is the same.

Rule 3: Irregular Agreement (Special Cases)

Many adjectives have irregular spelling changes for feminine or plural forms.

    bijou, caillou, chou, genou, hibou, joujou, pou (→ bijoux, cailloux...)
  • -al: Some exceptions just add -s:
    bal, carnaval, festival, récital, régal (→ bals, carnavals...)
  • -ail: Most just add -s (un détail → des détails), but a few change to -aux:
    travail → travaux, vitrail → vitraux

Rule 4: Adjective Placement

In French, adjectives can go before or after the noun. The position depends on the *type* of adjective.

1. Descriptive Adjectives (Usually AFTER the noun)

These describe a quality (color, shape, mood, nationality, etc.). They answer the question, "What kind of...?"

  • un livre vert (a green book)
  • la voiture rouge (the red car)
  • un chat noir (a black cat)
  • un étudiant intelligent (an intelligent student)

The 'BAGS' Exception (BEFORE the noun)

A few common descriptive adjectives are special and go before the noun. You can remember them with the acronym BAGS:

  • Beauty: beau (beautiful), joli (pretty)
  • Age: jeune (young), vieux (old), nouveau (new)
  • Goodness: bon (good), mauvais (bad)
  • Size: Example: une belle fille (a beautiful girl), un jeune homme (a young man)

2. Limiting Adjectives (Always BEFORE the noun)

These adjectives don't describe a quality. They "limit" or specify *which* noun you're talking about (e.g., "my", "this", "three"). They always go before the noun.

Type Function Example (French) Translation
Demonstrative Point to a noun ce livre, cette table this book, this table
Possessive Show ownership mon livre, ma maison my book, my house
Numerical Count nouns trois chats three cats
Indefinite Unspecified amount plusieurs personnes several people
Interrogative Ask "which?" or "what?" quel jour ? which day?
Exclamative Exclaim "what a...!" quelle surprise ! what a surprise!

Easy Memory Trick

  • If it describes (color, size, shape, mood) → AFTER noun (e.g., le livre rouge).
  • If it's BAGS (Beauty, Age, Goodness, Size) → BEFORE noun (e.g., le grand livre).
  • If it limits/points/counts (my, this, three, which) → BEFORE noun (e.g., mon livre, ce livre, deux livres).

Rule 5: Adjectives with Prepositions

Some adjectives require a specific preposition (like à or de) to connect to the next part of the sentence, especially after the verb être (to be).

1. Adjectives + à (to, at)

Used for ability, readiness, determination, or being busy.

French Meaning Example
être prêt à to be ready to Je suis prêt à partir. (I'm ready to leave.)
être habitué à to be used to Je suis habitué à me lever tôt. (I'm used to waking up early.)
être décidé à to be determined to Elle est décidée à réussir. (She's determined to succeed.)

Tip:

If it’s about what you can do, want to do, or are about to do → use à.

2. Adjectives + de (of, about)

Used for emotions, feelings, opinions, or being forced/allowed.

French Meaning Example
être content de to be happy to Je suis content de te voir. (I'm happy to see you.)
être fier de to be proud of Elle est fière de son fils. (She's proud of her son.)
être fatigué de to be tired of Il est fatigué de travailler. (He's tired of working.)

Tip:

If it’s about how you feel or an emotion, it usually takes de.

3. Adjectives + pour (for, to)

Used with assez (enough) or trop (too) to express "enough to..." or "too... to...".

French Meaning Example
assez ... pour ... enough to Il est assez fort pour soulever ça. (He's strong enough to lift that.)
trop ... pour too ... to Il est trop petit pour conduire. (He's too small to drive.)

Rule 6: Adverbs (Modifying Adjectives)

Adverbs like très (very), trop (too), and assez (quite/fairly) go before the adjective they modify.

  • très petit (very small)
  • trop grand (too big)
  • assez joli (quite pretty)

Adjective Rules: Quick Summary

Les Adverbes

What is an Adverb?

An adverb describes or gives more detail about a verb (action), an adjective (description), or another adverb. It answers questions like: How? When? Where? How much?

The Golden Rule: Adverbs are invariable. They never change. Unlike adjectives, they don't need to agree with a noun's gender or number.
  • Il court vite. (He runs fast.)
  • Elle court vite. (She runs fast.)
  • Ils courent vite. (They run fast.)

How to Form Adverbs

Most adverbs are formed from adjectives, similar to adding "-ly" in English.

Rule 1: Feminine Adjective + -ment

This is the most common rule. Take the masculine adjective, find its feminine form, and add -ment.

Adjective (Masc) Feminine Form Adverb English
poli polie poliment politely
lent lente lentement slowly
heureux heureuse heureusement happily, fortunately

Rule 2: Adjectives Ending in a Vowel, -ant, or -ent

These have shortcuts:

  • If the masculine adjective ends in -e (like calme), just add -ment.
    → calme → calmement (calmly)
  • If the adjective ends in -ant, change it to -amment.
    → constant → constamment (constantly)
  • If the adjective ends in -ent, change it to -emment.
    → récent → récemment (recently)

Common Irregular Adverbs

Some of the most common adverbs don't follow the -ment rule and must be memorized.

Adverb English (Related Adjective)
bien well (bon - good)
mal badly (mauvais - bad)
vite fast, quickly (rapide - fast)
beaucoup a lot, much, very
trop too, too much
peu little
très very

Types of Adverbs

Type Answers... Examples
Manner How? bien (well), mal (badly), vite (fast), poliment (politely)
Frequency How often? toujours (always), souvent (often), parfois (sometimes), jamais (never)
Place Where? ici (here), là (there), dehors (outside), partout (everywhere)
Time When? maintenant (now), hier (yesterday), bientôt (soon), déjà (already)
Quantity How much? très (very), peu (little), trop (too much), beaucoup (a lot)
Evaluation Opinion? absolument (absolutely), peut-être (maybe), vraiment (really)

Where to Put Adverbs (Word Order)

(a) Modifying a Verb: Place it after the verb.
→ Je travaille beaucoup. (I work a lot.)
→ J’ai bien compris. (I understood well. *After the first verb.*)
(b) Modifying an Adjective or Adverb: Place it before that word.
→ Je suis très fatigué. (I am very tired.)
→ Il parle assez lentement. (He speaks quite slowly.)
(c) Modifying a Whole Sentence: Place it at the beginning or end.
→ Heureusement, il est venu. (Fortunately, he came.)

In Short: Adverbs vs. Adjectives

  • Adjectives = CHANGE to match the noun's gender and number.
  • Adverbs = NEVER CHANGE, often end in -ment, and describe *how*, *where*, *when*, or *how much*.

L' Article

What Are Articles?

Articles are the small words before nouns (like "a," "an," or "the"). In French, they change depending on the noun's:

  • Gender: Masculine or Feminine
  • Number: Singular or Plural
  • First Letter: Starts with a vowel or silent "h"
  • Context: Specific (the), general (a/an), or partial (some)

There are 3 main types: Definite (the), Indefinite (a/an), and Partitive (some).

1. Definite Articles (The)

Used for something specific or known. The English equivalent is "the".

Type Singular Plural Example
Masculine le les le livre (the book)
Feminine la les la table (the table)
Vowel / Silent 'h' l' les l'arbre (the tree), l'école (the school)
In short: le, la, l', and les all mean "the".

2. Indefinite Articles (A / An / Some)

Used for something non-specific or countable (e.g., "a book," "some books").

Type Singular Plural Example
Masculine un des un livre (a book), des livres (some books)
Feminine une des une pomme (an apple), des pommes (some apples)
In short: un / une mean "a/an". des means "some" (for countable items).

3. Partitive Articles (Some of...)

Used for uncountable things, like food, liquids, or materials (e.g., "some water," "some cheese").

Type Singular Plural Example
Masculine du des du pain (some bread)
Feminine de la des de la soupe (some soup)
Vowel / Silent 'h' de l' des de l'eau (some water), des épinards (some spinach)
In short: du, de la, de l' mean "some" (for uncountable items).

Bonus Rule: Negation with de

In negative sentences (with ne...pas), all indefinite and partitive articles (un, une, des, du, de la, de l') usually become just de (or d' before a vowel).

  • J’ai un chien. → Je n’ai pas de chien. (I don't have a dog.)
  • Je bois du café. → Je ne bois pas de café. (I don't drink coffee.)
  • Elle a des amis. → Elle n’a pas d'amis. (She doesn't have any friends.)

Summary of All Articles

Gender/Number Definite (the) Indefinite (a/an/some) Partitive (some of...)
Masculine singular le un du
Feminine singular la une de la
Starts with vowel / 'h' l' (uses un / une) de l'
Plural (any gender) les des des

How to Think About It

English Thought French Article Example
"The" specific thing / Liking in general le, la, l', les J'aime le chocolat. (I like chocolate.)
"A/An" one countable thing un, une J’ai une voiture. (I have a car.)
"Some" of an uncountable thing du, de la, de l' Je veux du pain. (I want some bread.)
"Some" plural countable things des J’ai des amis. (I have some friends.)
"Not any" / "No" de / d' Je n'ai pas de chien. (I don't have a dog.)

Les Conjonctions

What Are Conjunctions?

Conjunctions are words that connect parts of a sentence. They join words, phrases, or even whole sentences together.

Example: "I like coffee and tea." (J’aime le café et le thé.)
→ "and" is joining "coffee" and "tea".

Two Types of Conjunctions

  • Coordinating conjunctions: Join equal things (A + B).
  • Subordinating conjunctions: Join unequal things (A depends on B).

1. Coordinating Conjunctions (Equal Parts)

These connect two words, phrases, or clauses that have the same function. You could swap them, and the sentence still makes sense.

French Meaning Example
et and Il est jeune et beau. (He’s young and handsome.)
ou or Tu veux du café ou du thé ? (Do you want coffee or tea?)
mais but J’aime le café, mais pas le thé. (I like coffee, but not tea.)
donc so / therefore Il pleut, donc je reste ici. (It’s raining, so I’m staying here.)
car because (formal) Je pars, car je suis fatigué. (I’m leaving because I’m tired.)
ni ... ni neither ... nor Je n’aime ni le café ni le thé. (I like neither coffee nor tea.)

Trick to remember:

The main coordinating conjunctions are: et, ou, mais, donc, car, ni.

2. Subordinating Conjunctions (Unequal Parts)

These connect a main idea (main clause) with a dependent idea (subordinate clause). The dependent part can't stand alone as a full sentence.

French Meaning Example
que that Elle sait que tu l’as fait. (She knows that you did it.)
quand when Dis-moi quand tu es prêt. (Tell me when you’re ready.)
si if / whether Je viendrai si tu veux. (I’ll come if you want.)
parce que because Je pleure parce que je suis triste. (I cry because I’m sad.)
lorsque when (formal) Lorsque j’étais enfant... (When I was a child...)
puisque since / as Puisque tu es ici, aide-moi. (Since you’re here, help me.)
pendant que while Il lit pendant que je cuisine. (He reads while I cook.)

3. Conjunctive Phrases (Multi-word)

Sometimes, a conjunction is a phrase of two or more words.

French Meaning Example
afin que so that / in order to Je pars tôt afin que je puisse dormir. (I’m leaving early so that I can sleep.)
bien que although Bien que fatigué, il travaille. (Although tired, he works.)
à condition que provided that Tu peux venir à condition que tu sois calme. (You can come provided you stay calm.)
de peur que for fear that Elle chuchote de peur que... (She whispers for fear that...)

How to Tell the Difference

Type Relationship Can You Swap the Clauses? Example
Coordinating Equal Ideas (A + B) ✅ Yes Il est beau et jeune.
Subordinating Dependent Idea (A → B) ❌ No Elle sait que tu l’as fait.

Easy Way to Remember

  • Coordinating = "and, or, but" → They balance two equal ideas.
  • Subordinating = "because, when, if" → One idea depends on the other.

Les Noms (Nouns)

What Are Nouns?

A noun (un nom) is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea.

English French
a book un livre
a cat un chat
a chair une chaise
an idea une idée

Rule 1: Every French Noun Has a Gender

This is the big difference from English. Every single noun is either masculine (like "he") or feminine (like "she"). This is purely grammatical.

  • le livre (the book) → masculine
  • la table (the table) → feminine

The Golden Rule: How to Remember Gender

Always learn a noun together with its article (like un or une).

❌ Wrong

livre = book

✅ Correct

un livre = a book

This helps your brain store them as one unit (like "un-livre") so you automatically know the gender.

Common Gender Patterns

There's no perfect logic, but some patterns can help you guess.

💙 Commonly Masculine Categories
  • Days, months, seasons: le lundi, le mars, l'hiver
  • Languages: le français, l'anglais
  • Trees, metals, measurements: le chêne (oak), le fer (iron), le kilomètre
💗 Commonly Feminine Categories
  • Sciences & subjects: la chimie, la biologie, la musique
  • Nouns ending in -e (often, not always!): la table, la chaise
  • Countries ending in -e: la France, la Chine, l'Italie
  • Abstract ideas: la liberté, la justice

Common Endings That Hint at Gender

Usually Masculine Usually Feminine
-age → le fromage (cheese) -tion → la nation (nation)
-ment → le moment (moment) -sion → la décision (decision)
-eau → le bateau (boat) -té → la beauté (beauty)
-isme → le tourisme (tourism) -ette → la baguette (baguette)
-oir → le miroir (mirror) -ure → la voiture (car)
-in → le jardin (garden) -ade → la salade (salad)
-on → le poisson (fish) -ance / -ence → la chance / la différence

⚠️ Exceptions exist! E.g., la plage (the beach) ends in -age but is feminine.

Rule 2: Nouns for People & Animals

When a noun refers to a person or animal, it can have different masculine and feminine forms.

English Masculine Feminine
friend un ami une amie
student un étudiant une étudiante
cat un chat une chatte
waiter / waitress un serveur une serveuse

Rule 3: Singular & Plural Forms

Like English, nouns can be singular (one thing) or plural (more than one). The default rule is to add an -s.

Singular Plural English
un livre des livres (some) books
une pomme des pommes (some) apples
⚠️ Exception: If a word already ends in -s, -x, or -z, you don’t add another -s for the plural.
(e.g., un choix → des choix, un nez → des nez)

Rule 4: Articles Must Match the Noun

You must always use an article, and it must match the noun's gender and number.

Meaning Masculine Feminine Plural
the le / l' la / l' les
a / an un une des
some (of) du / de l' de la / de l' des

Rule 5: Contractions with à & de

When the prepositions à (to/at) or de (of/from) come before le or les, they are forced to combine.

Combination Result Example
à + le au Je vais au parc. (I'm going to the park.)
à + les aux Je parle aux enfants. (I talk to the children.)
de + le du Je viens du cinéma. (I come from the cinema.)
de + les des Le livre des enfants. (The children's book.)

Nouns: Quick Summary

Concept Rule Example
Gender All nouns are masculine or feminine le livre (m), la table (f)
Article Always learn noun with its article Learn "un livre", not just "livre"
Plural Usually add -s un livre → des livres
People/Animals Gender of noun can change un ami (m) / une amie (f)
Guessing Gender Based on ending or category -tion is usually fem, -ment is usually masc
Contractions à/de + le/les combine à + le → au, de + le → du

Les Prépositions

What Are Prepositions?

Prepositions are small link words that show relationships between things—usually in place, time, or connection. They act as bridges between two parts of a sentence.

Example 1: Je vais à Paris. (I’m going to Paris.)

Example 2: une tasse de café. (a cup of coffee.)

They don’t mean much by themselves, but connect meanings. The two most important are à (to/at/in) and de (from/of/about).

1. The Two Most Important: à and de

➤ à (usually "to", "at", or "in")

Meaning Example
Going to a place Je vais à Paris. (I’m going to Paris.)
Being at a place Je suis à l’école. (I’m at school.)
Talking to someone Je parle à Marie. (I’m talking to Marie.)

Contraction Tip:

à + le → au (Je vais au cinéma.)

à + les → aux (Je parle aux enfants.)

➤ de (usually "from", "of", or "about")

Meaning Example
Coming from Je viens de Paris. (I come from Paris.)
Showing possession Le livre de Marie. (Marie’s book.)
Describing content une tasse de café. (a cup of coffee.)
Talking about parler de musique. (to talk about music.)

Contraction Tip:

de + le → du (le livre du garçon.)

de + les → des (la maison des voisins.)

2. Prepositions of Time

French English Example
avant before avant le dîner (before dinner)
après after après l’école (after school)
pendant during pendant la nuit (during the night)
depuis since / for ...depuis 5 ans (for 5 years)
jusque until / up to jusqu’à demain (until tomorrow)

3. Prepositions of Place

French English Example
dans in dans la boîte (in the box)
sur on sur la table (on the table)
sous under sous la chaise (under the chair)
devant in front of devant la maison (in front of the house)
derrière behind derrière la porte (behind the door)
entre between entre les deux (between the two)
vers toward vers la sortie (toward the exit)
chez at someone’s place chez moi (at my house)
contre against contre le mur (against the wall)

4. Prepositions of Purpose or Relationship

French English Example
pour for un cadeau pour toi (a gift for you)
sans without sans sucre (without sugar)
selon according to selon moi (according to me)
avec with avec mes amis (with my friends)
par by / through fait par ma mère (made by my mother)
malgré despite malgré la pluie (despite the rain)
sauf except sauf toi (except you)

5. Prepositional Phrases

Sometimes French combines words (especially with de) to make one "preposition-like" idea.

French English Example
près de near J’habite près de la gare. (I live near the station.)
loin de far from Il est loin de moi. (He is far from me.)
à côté de next to ...à côté de la poste. (next to the post office)
en face de opposite / facing ...en face de moi. (opposite me)
au lieu de instead of ...au lieu de partir. (instead of leaving)

6. Key Difference (English vs. French)

⚠️ Warning: You can’t always translate prepositions 1-to-1.

French often uses a different preposition than English, or uses one when English doesn't. This must be learned through practice.

  • "I’m good at math." → Je suis bon en maths.
  • "I’m interested in music." → Je m’intéresse à la musique.
  • "Think about it." → Pense à ça.

7. Pronouns After Prepositions

When you use a pronoun after a preposition, you must use the "disjunctive" (or stress) pronouns.

English Pronoun Example
me moi avec moi (with me)
you (sing.) toi pour toi (for you)
him lui sans lui (without him)
her elle chez elle (at her place)
us nous avec nous (with us)
you (pl.) vous pour vous (for you)
them (m.) eux devant eux (in front of them)
them (f.) elles derrière elles (behind them)

Prepositions: Quick Summary

Category Common Words Example
Direction / Place à, dans, sur, sous, chez, entre, vers Je vais à Paris. / Il est sous la table.
Origin / Possession de, du, des Le livre du garçon. / Je viens de France.
Time avant, après, pendant, depuis, jusque Après le dîner, je dors.
Purpose / Relation pour, sans, avec, selon, par, contre Un cadeau pour toi. / Fait par moi.

Les Pronoms (Pronouns)

What Are Pronouns?

A pronoun is a word that replaces a noun (a person, place, or thing) to avoid repetition.

Example: Marie est gentille. Marie aide Paul.

→ Elle est gentille. Elle aide Paul.

("Marie is kind. She helps Paul.") Here, elle (she) replaces "Marie".

Two Big Groups of Pronouns

  • Personal Pronouns: Change depending on their role (I, you, he, me, him, etc.).
  • Impersonal Pronouns: Have special jobs and don't refer to a specific person (it, this, that, who, what, etc.).

1. Personal Pronouns

These refer to people (or things) and change based on their job in the sentence. There are five main types.

A. Subject Pronouns (Who does the action)

Used before the verb to show who is doing the action.

English French Example
I je Je mange. (I eat.)
you (informal) tu Tu parles. (You speak.)
he / she / one il / elle / on Il dort. (He sleeps.)
we nous Nous sortons. (We go out.)
you (formal/plural) vous Vous lisez. (You read.)
they (m. / f.) ils / elles Elles étudient. (They study.)

B. Reflexive Pronouns (Do something to yourself)

Used with reflexive verbs, where the action "reflects back" on the subject.

Pronoun Meaning Example
me / m' myself Je me lave. (I wash myself.)
te / t' yourself Tu te lèves. (You get up.)
se / s' himself / herself / itself / themselves Il se rase. (He shaves.)
nous ourselves Nous nous habillons. (We get dressed.)
vous yourselves Vous vous reposez. (You rest.)

C. Direct Object Pronouns (Receive the action)

Replaces the noun that *directly* receives the verb's action (answers "who?" or "what?").

Pronoun English Example
me / m' me Tu me vois. (You see me.)
te / t' you Je te vois. (I see you.)
le / l' him / it (masc.) Je le vois. (I see him/it.)
la / l' her / it (fem.) Je la vois. (I see her/it.)
nous us Il nous voit. (He sees us.)
vous you Je vous vois. (I see you.)
les them Je les vois. (I see them.)

Example: "Il mange la pomme." (He eats the apple.)

→ "Il la mange." (He eats it.)

D. Indirect Object Pronouns (To/For someone)

Replaces a noun that receives the action *indirectly*. Always answers "to whom?" or "for whom?".

Pronoun English Example
me / m' to me Tu me parles. (You talk to me.)
te / t' to you Je te parle. (I talk to you.)
lui to him / to her Je lui parle. (I talk to him/her.)
nous to us Il nous parle. (He talks to us.)
vous to you Je vous parle. (I talk to you.)
leur to them Je leur parle. (I talk to them.)

Example: "Je parle à Marie." (I am talking to Marie.)

→ "Je lui parle." (I am talking to her.)

E. Stressed (Disjunctive) Pronouns

Used for emphasis, after prepositions (like avec, pour, de), or by themselves.

Pronoun English Example
moi me C’est moi! (It's me!) / avec moi (with me)
toi you C'est toi? (Is that you?) / pour toi (for you)
lui him Je parle de lui. (I'm talking about him.)
elle her Je pense à elle. (I'm thinking of her.)
nous us Ils viennent avec nous. (They're coming with us.)
vous you C'est pour vous. (It's for you.)
eux them (masc.) sans eux (without them)
elles them (fem.) chez elles (at their place)

Personal Pronouns: Quick Summary

Function Example Use Pronouns
Subject Before the verb je, tu, il/elle/on, nous, vous, ils/elles
Reflexive With reflexive verbs me, te, se, nous, vous, se
Direct Object Replaces direct receiver (sees *what*?) me, te, le/la, nous, vous, les
Indirect Object Replaces "to/for" receiver (talks *to whom*?) me, te, lui, nous, vous, leur

2. Impersonal Pronouns

These pronouns have special grammar jobs and don't replace a specific person.

A. Adverbial Pronouns: y and en

Pronoun Replaces / Meaning Example
y "there" (a place) / "about it" (à + thing) Je vais à Paris. → J'y vais. (I'm going there.)
en "some" / "of it" (de + thing / quantity) J'ai des pommes. → J'en ai. (I have some.)

B. Demonstrative Pronouns (this one / that one)

Pronoun Meaning Example
celui / celle this one / that one (sing.) Celui-là est beau. (That one is beautiful.)
ceux / celles these / those (pl.) Celles-ci sont grandes. (These are tall.)

C. Impersonal Subjects: ce and il

Used for expressions without a clear "person" doing the action.

Expression Meaning / Use Example
Il pleut. Impersonal subject for weather It's raining.
C'est difficile. General "it/that is" expression It's hard. / That's hard.
Ce sont mes amis. Plural demonstrative with identifying info They are my friends.

D. Indefinite Demonstrative: ce, ceci, cela, ça

Used for general, non-specific things or ideas ("this," "that").

Pronoun Meaning Example
ce that / it (formal/general) C'est vrai. (That's true.)
ça that / this (colloquial) J'aime ça. (I like that.)
ceci this (formal, often written) Ceci est important. (This is important.)
cela that (formal) Cela peut aider. (That can help.)

Used for unknown or general quantities/people.

Pronoun Meaning Example

Replaces a noun that belongs to someone (e.g., "my book" → "mine").

Pronoun Use Example

Les Verbes (Verbs)

1. What Is a Verb?

A verb is an action word or a state word — something happening or being.

English French Type
I eat. Je mange. Action
She speaks. Elle parle. Action
We are happy. Nous sommes heureux. State

2. What Does "Conjugate" Mean?

In French, verbs change form (their "costume") depending on who is doing the action and when it's happening. This change is called conjugation.

A verb's form is determined by 5 elements. For now, the most important are Person, Number, and Tense.

3. The 5 Elements of Conjugation

A. Person & Number (Who does the action)

This shows who is doing the action (the subject). There are 6 "slots" for any verb.

Person Singular (One) Plural (Many)
1st (the speaker) je (I) nous (we)
2nd (person spoken to) tu (you, informal) vous (you, plural/formal)
3rd (person spoken about) il / elle (he / she) ils / elles (they)

B. Tense (When it happens)

This controls the time of the action.

Time English French
Present I eat je mange
Past I ate / I have eaten j'ai mangé
Future I will eat je mangerai

C. Mood (How the action is expressed)

This shows the speaker's attitude (e.g., stating a fact, giving a command, expressing doubt).

Mood Use Example
Indicative Stating facts (most common) I eat apples.
Imperative Giving commands Eat apples!
Subjunctive Expressing doubt/emotion I want you to eat apples.

D. Voice (Who receives the action)

Shows if the subject is *doing* the action or *receiving* it.

Voice English French
Active I eat the apple. Je mange la pomme.
Passive The apple is eaten by me. La pomme est mangée par moi.

4. How It Works: Root + Ending

Think of a conjugated verb as two parts: the root (which carries the meaning) and the ending (which tells you WHO and WHEN).

Let's use manger (to eat). The root is mang-.

Meaning French Root Ending (tells you...)
I eat je mange mang- -e (...who: I)
you eat tu manges mang- -es (...who: you)
he/she eats il mange mang- -e (...who: he/she)
we eat nous mangeons mang- -eons (...who: we)
you (pl.) eat vous mangez mang- -ez (...who: you pl.)
they eat ils mangent mang- -ent (...who: they)

5. Regular Conjugation Patterns (The 3 Families)

Every French verb, in its basic form (called the infinitive), ends in -er, -ir, or -re.

Ending Example Meaning Regular?
-er parler / manger to speak / to eat ✅ Regular
-ir finir to finish ✅ Regular
-re vendre to sell ✅ Regular

⚠️ Some verbs are irregular and don't follow the pattern, like:

  • être (to be)
  • avoir (to have)
  • aller (to go)
  • faire (to do/make)

For regular verbs, just find the stem (cut off the ending) and add the right ending for that family.

Regular -ir verb (e.g., finir)

Stem: fin-

Person Ending Result
je -is je finis
tu -is tu finis
il/elle -it il/elle finit
nous -issons nous finissons
vous -issez vous finissez
ils/elles -issent ils/elles finissent

Regular -re verb (e.g., vendre)

Stem: vend-

Person Ending Result
je -s je vends
tu -s tu vends
il/elle (nothing) il/elle vend
nous -ons nous vendons
vous -ez vous vendez
ils/elles -ent ils/elles vendent

6. Why French Does This

In English, we use "helping words" to show who and when:

→ I eat, he eats, I will eat, I am eating.

In French, instead of using lots of extra words, they change the ending of the main verb to show that same information.

The verb ending itself contains the meaning of "who" and "when". That's why one French verb can have so many different forms.

Mood how it's said (fact, command, doubt) je mange (fact) / Mange! (command)
